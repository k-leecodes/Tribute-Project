Langston Hughes

Langston Hughes born February 1, 1901 in Joplin, Missouri was an American poet and writer who was a prominent figure in the Harlem Renaissance. He made the African American experience the subject of his writing, which ranged from poetry and plays to novels. Some of his most famous pieces of work include "The Negro Speaks of Rivers" (1921) "Mother to Son" (1922) "Dreams" (1922) and "The Weary Blues" (1925)

"Hold fast to dreams, for if dreams die, life is a broken-winged bird that cannot fly". - Langston Hughes

    A Timeline of Langston Hughes's Life and Accomplishments:

  • 1901 - February 2 - Langston Hughes is born in Joplin, Missouri. His parents are Carrie Langston Hughes and James Nathaniel Hughes.
  • 1903 - Hughes moves in with his grandmother Mary Langston in Lawrence, Kansas after his parents seperate.
  • 1914 - Hughes's grandmother passes away which forces him to move to Lincoln, Illinois to live with his mother and his new stepfather Homer Clark.
  • 1916 - Hughes moves to Cleveland, Ohio after his stepfather finds work in the steel mill industry.
  • 1920 - June - After graduating from high school, Hughes travels to Mexico to be with his father. On his journey to Mexico he writes the poem "The Negroes Speaks of Rivers"
  • 1921 - June - In what would become his signature poem, "The Negroes Speaks of Rivers" is published for the first time in The Crisis.
  • 1921 - September - Hughes moves to New York City after being accepted at Columbia University. His father agreed to pay for school as long as he studies engineering. Hughes ends up dropping out of college after two semesters.
  • 1923 - Hughes takes a job on the ship S.S Malone. He traveled for six months before taking a job as a cook in Paris.
  • 1924 - November - While he is working as a busboy at the Wardman Park Hotel in Washington, D.C., Hughes sees the poet Vachel Lindsay sitting at a table. Hughes gives him some of his own poems and Lindsay puts him in contact with the Knopf Publishing Company which leads to Hughes getting his first big break as a writer.
  • 1926 - Hughes' first book of poetry, "The Weary Blues" is published by Knopf Publishing Company. The book is an amazing success and Hughes makes enough income off of it to return to college, this time at Lincoln University in Pennsylvania.
  • 1929 - Hughes graduates from Lincoln University with a Bachelor of Arts degree. After he graduates, he moves to New York City, which he calls home for the rest of his life.
  • 1935 - Because of his outstanding creative writing, Hughes wins the Guggenheim Fellowship. This gives him money that allows him to concentrate on writing and soon after that, his play "The Mulatto" opens on Broadway.
  • 1937 - Working for the Baltimore Afro-American newspaper, Hughes is a reporter at the Spanish Civil War in Spain. He met Ernest Hemingway there and he published his book about the Spanish Civil War, "For Whom the Bell Tolls" three years later.
  • 1946 - After over 20 years of writing poems, plays, screenplays, and novels, Hughes is elected to the National Institute of Arts and Letters. It is a very distinguished institute that only allows 250 people to be in at one time.
  • 1947 - Hughes accepts his first teaching job teaching creative writing at Atlanta University. Two years later he spends a year teaching at the University of Chicago.
  • 1960 - In honor of his amazing career as a writer, Hughes wins the Spingarn Medal from the NAACP. The medal is given once a year to honor an African American for outstanding achievement.
  • 1965 - May 22 - Langston Hughes dies after having surgery for prostate cancer. His ashes are buried in the floor of the Arthur Schomberg Center for Research in Black Culture in Harlem.
